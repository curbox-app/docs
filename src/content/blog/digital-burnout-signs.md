We talk a lot about physical burnout — the exhaustion after weeks of 12-hour days. But there is a quieter, stranger kind of burnout that most of us never name: digital burnout.

It is not about working too hard. It is about being *on* too much. Notifications, feeds, group chats, emails, alerts — the modern smartphone creates a state of perpetual, low-grade stimulation that never fully switches off. And after months or years of this, the nervous system pays a price.

Here are five signs that you might be experiencing it.

---

## 1. You Feel Exhausted After "Relaxing" on Your Phone

You spend an hour scrolling social media to wind down after work. But instead of feeling rested, you feel... hollow. Vaguely agitated. More tired than before you started.

This is not just a feeling. Research by psychologists at the University of British Columbia found that participants who reduced their social media use to 30 minutes per day reported significant reductions in depression and loneliness after just three weeks.

The key insight: passive consumption of social media — scrolling, watching, reacting — activates the brain's threat-detection systems. Your amygdala is scanning every post for social signals: *Am I included? Am I behind? Are people judging me?* That is not rest. That is surveillance.

---

## 2. You Cannot Be Bored

You reach for your phone whenever you have ten unoccupied seconds. Waiting for the kettle. Standing in a lift. Sitting at a red light (please, no).

Boredom has gotten a terrible reputation. But boredom is not emptiness — it is the mind's signal that it wants to *wander*. And mind-wandering, as neuroscientist Mary Helen Immordino-Yang's research shows, is essential for self-reflection, creativity, and emotional processing.

The inability to be bored is a symptom of digital burnout: your brain has been so thoroughly trained to expect stimulation that the absence of it feels unbearable.

---

## 3. Your Attention Span Has Fragmented

You start reading an article. Halfway down the first paragraph, you are already thinking about checking something else. You watch a video and feel compelled to skip ahead. You start a task at work and find your hands reaching for your phone before your conscious mind has even registered the impulse.

This is not a character flaw. It is a trained behaviour. Every notification, every variable-reward scroll, every autoplay video has been conditioning your attention for rapid switching. The good news: attention is trainable in the other direction too.

---

## 4. You Are Sleeping Poorly — and You Know Why

You know you should not look at your phone after 10pm. You know the blue light disrupts melatonin. You know the emotional content of your feed keeps your brain activated. You know all of this, and you still do it.

This gap between knowledge and behaviour is itself a sign of burnout. When we are in a healthy, regulated state, our executive function — the prefrontal cortex — can make and keep commitments. When we are burned out, the impulsive, habitual systems take over.

---

## 5. Real-World Experiences Feel Understimulating

You are at a beautiful dinner with people you love, and part of you is somewhere else — half-composing the caption for the photo you already took. Real conversations feel slow. Nature feels quiet in a way that is almost uncomfortable.

When the digital world's density of stimulus — infinite novelty, perfect lighting, instant reaction — becomes your baseline, the analogue world cannot compete. This is perhaps the most concerning sign, because it means the technology is reshaping your perception of what is worth experiencing.

---

## What to Do

Recognition is the first step. If several of these signs resonated, it does not mean something is wrong with you — it means you have been swimming in the same water as everyone else.

Practical first steps:

1. **Track your usage honestly.** Before you can change it, see it clearly. Curbox's analytics can show you the truth without editorialising.
2. **Create phone-free spaces.** Bedroom. Dining table. First 30 minutes after waking.
3. **Replace, don't just restrict.** The urge to scroll is usually a need in disguise — for rest, connection, stimulation, or escape. Find analogue ways to meet those needs.
4. **Start small.** A 20-minute daily limit on one app is more sustainable than a grand detox that collapses on day three.

Digital burnout is a symptom of a system designed to burn you out. Recognising that — and making small, deliberate choices against it — is a quiet act of resistance.
